Cuba libre is a refreshing cocktail made with cola, lime juice, and rum. It originated in Cuba and is a popular drink in many parts of the world.

<!--more-->

**Cuba libre recipe**

- 120 ml cola
- 50 ml white rum
- 10 ml Fresh lime juice


Build all ingredients in a highball glass filled with ice. Garnish with lime wedge.

![](/cocktail-images/cuba_libre.jpeg)

## How to build a Cuba Libre properly

1. Fill a chilled highball glass almost to the top with fresh ice. Plenty of ice matters here: it keeps the drink crisp and slows dilution so the cola stays lively.
2. Pour in the white rum first, then add the fresh lime juice. Adding the citrus before the soda helps it blend more evenly through the drink.
3. Top with chilled cola, pouring gently to preserve carbonation. If possible, pour down the inside of the glass rather than directly onto the ice.
4. Give the drink one brief, gentle stir—just enough to combine the rum, lime, and cola without knocking out too much fizz.
5. Garnish with a lime wedge. For a brighter aroma, lightly squeeze the wedge over the surface before dropping it in or resting it on the rim.
6. Serve immediately while the bubbles are sharp and the drink is ice-cold.

## What the Cuba Libre tastes like

The Cuba Libre is more than rum and cola with a lime garnish. The lime is what changes the balance, sharpening the sweetness of the soda and lifting the rum’s vanilla, cane, and light tropical notes. The result is refreshing, slightly zesty, and easy-drinking, with a familiar cola backbone and a cleaner finish than a basic rum-and-coke.

## Getting the best serve

Because this drink is built directly in the glass, temperature makes a big difference. Use cold ingredients and solid ice, and avoid over-stirring. A fresh wedge is worth it: bottled citrus tends to flatten the drink. If you want a drier version, use a cola with less sweetness or increase the lime slightly to make the finish snappier.

## Cuba Libre origins and bar trivia

The Cuba Libre is generally linked to Cuba in the early 1900s, around the period following the Spanish–American War. The most widely repeated story is that American cola met Cuban rum in Havana, followed by a toast of “¡Cuba libre!” meaning “Free Cuba.” Exact documentation is a little fuzzy, but that origin story is the most credible and enduring account. Its staying power comes from simplicity: it’s one of the world’s best-known two-plus-one builds, where a small hit of citrus makes all the difference.

## A booze-free Cuba Libre-style highball

For a non-alcoholic version, use a zero-proof white rum alternative or simply build the drink with cola, fresh lime juice, and extra ice. A tiny dash of vanilla extract or a few drops of molasses syrup can add some rum-like depth. Keep the lime generous so it still tastes bright rather than just sweet soda.
